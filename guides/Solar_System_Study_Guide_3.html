17. Red Dwarf Habitability Analysis (16 points total)
All of the exoplanets in this year's rules orbit red dwarfs, which are smaller, less massive, and dimmer than the Sun. In this question, we'll explore some of the ways red dwarfs can impact the habitability of planets around them.
(a) Compared to our Sun's habitable zone, would the habitable zone around a red dwarf be closer or farther away? Justify your answer by either writing a few short sentences or referencing the equation for planetary equilibrium temperature. [2 pts]
(b) Planets in the habitable zone of red dwarfs have a significant likelihood of being tidally locked to their parent star, but none of the planets in our Solar System are tidally locked to the Sun. Building off your answer to the previous part, why might planets in the habitable zones of red dwarfs be more susceptible to being tidally locked? Hint: think about the shape (and especially slope) of the function f(r) = 1/r¬≤ at both large and small values of r. [3 pts]
(c) Challenge: Consider an alternate universe in which the force of gravity scales with 1/r¬≥ instead of 1/r¬≤. Do you think objects would get tidally locked faster or slower than in our universe? [4 pts]
(d) A tidally locked planet would have one side facing its parent star at all times, while the other side is in constant darkness. What are some ways life could survive in such an environment? Are there any ways that the planet could redistribute the heat? Note: this is intentionally written as an open-ended question, and you are encouraged to be creative (and imaginative) in your response. A variety of answers will be accepted. [3 pts]
(e) Red dwarfs emit a significant amount of ultraviolet radiation. Photons in this portion of the electromagnetic spectrum have energies comparable to the energy stored in chemical bonds, like the ones that make up proteins or DNA. Do you think this presence of this radiation would help or hinder the development of life around red dwarfs? [2 pts]
(f) Red dwarfs also "live" much longer than our Sun. Based on your knowledge of how (biological) life evolves, do you think this extra time would help or hinder the development of life around red dwarfs? [2 pts]
Answers:
(a) Closer. Red dwarfs are cooler and smaller, so in order for a planet around it to have the same temperature as Earth, it'll have to be closer. Imagine a scenario where you have two heaters during the wintertime. If one of them is weaker than the other, you'll have to stand closer to it to feel the same level of warmth as the stronger heater.
(b) The force of gravity is proportional to 1/r¬≤. So, the closer you get to the star, the stronger the force of gravity, and it stronger at a faster and faster rate as you get closer. As a result, when you're close to the parent star, you'll have a much bigger difference between the force the near and far sides of the planet experience, and it is this difference in gravitational force that drives tidal locking. A habitable planet around a red dwarf will be very close to its parent star (for the reasons outlined in the part above), so it will get tidally locked faster.
(c) A graph of 1/r¬≥ will have an even more pronounced curvature when close to the parent star, which would increase the tidal force a planet would experience, making it become tidally locked faster.
(d) Answers will vary dramatically; this was meant to be a question where students can come up with whatever ideas they want, and as long as it seemed like they put some thought into it, they got points for it.
(e) Hinder. The high-energy radiation would break bonds in complicated biological molecules (like DNA) needed for life, leading to errors (like mutations). Students who put that it could help life got points if it seemed like they thought about it a lot (for example, one team mentioned that this could lead to photocatalysis and help make molecules more easily).
(f) Help. Life takes time to develop, and the more time you give it, the greater the probability is that something will happen.
Red dwarf stars present unique challenges and opportunities for habitability, requiring careful consideration of orbital mechanics, atmospheric dynamics, and stellar evolution.
18. Mars Atmospheric Loss (12 points total)
Liquid water is thought to be essential for life as we know it. However, in order for water to exist as a liquid in a stable form, the pressure must be higher than 611.66 Pa. At some point Mars had an atmosphere substantial enough to support such a pressure, but most of it has since disappeared.
(a) 611.66 Pa is a very specific number, and it comes from a famous "point" on the pressure-temperature phase diagram for water. What is it? Hint: In case this number is more recognizable in other units, 611.66 Pa is equivalent to 6.1166 mbar or 0.0060366 atm. [2 pts]
(b) At a given temperature, do you think it is easier for light molecules (like hydrogen) or heavier molecules (like carbon dioxide) to escape from the atmosphere through this processes? [2 pts]
(c) For a given molecule, do you think it is easier for it to escape at high temperatures or low temperatures through this process? [2 pts]
(d) It turns out that this thermal process can only explain why Mars lost its hydrogen and helium. What do scientists think caused the other compounds to escape? [3 pts]
(e) One of the ways scientists supported this theory was by studying the relative abundances of two isotopes of argon, which is a Noble gas. From a chemistry perspective, why would they choose to focus on a Noble gas? [3 pts]
Answers:
(a) It is the pressure corresponding to the triple point of water.
(b) Light molecules. At a given temperature, light molecules will have a higher average speed, so a higher fraction of them will have a velocity high enough to escape the atmosphere.
(c) High temperatures. The higher temperatures will increase the average kinetic energy of the molecules in the gas.
(d) Several mechanisms probably played a role (e.g., solar wind/radiation, impacts). Sputtering due to solar wind/radiation after Mars lost its magnetic field is probably the dominant cause.
(e) Noble gases are inert (i.e., they do not easily react with other elements). So, the scientists would not have to worry about it being chemically stored on the planet, say, in a rock.
Mars's atmospheric loss is a complex process involving thermal escape, solar wind sputtering, and impact erosion, studied through isotopic analysis of noble gases.
19. Titan Subsurface Ocean (12 points total)
In addition to having stable bodies of liquid on its surface, Titan is also thought to have a subsurface ocean consisting primarily of liquid water. In this question, we'll begin by examining some of the evidence for a subsurface ocean on Titan before thinking more about how such a subsurface ocean could remain liquid even when Titan is so cold.
(a) Radar mapping carried out by the Cassini mission has shown geologic features on the surface of Titan changing positions by tens of kilometers over the course of only a few years. Why might this support the idea of a subsurface ocean? [2 pts]
(b) Furthermore, the Huygens lander detected extremely low-frequency radio waves during its descent. Why would the detection of these radio waves support the idea of a subsurface ocean? [2 pts]
(c) Challenge: From a thermodynamics perspective, why would adding ammonia decrease the freezing point of water? Frame your answer in terms of the entropic barrier that arises when the mixture starts to freeze. [5 pts]
(d) What is the lowest temperature the freezing point can go in this water-ammonia system? At this temperature, what percent of the subsurface ocean will consist of ammonia, by mole? [3 pts]
Answers:
(a) Landmarks shifting so much in such a short amount of time suggests the crust is disconnected from the rest of the interior of the planet (maybe a subsurface ocean!)
(b) Titan's low conductivity icy surface means that these radio waves readily penetrate the crust rather than being reflected. So, for them to resonate they must be reflected by something in Titan, which is most likely the high conductivity water‚Äìammonia subsurface ocean.
(c) As the mixture freezes, the water freezes first, increasing the concentration of the ammonia in the remaining liquid water. Freezing a substance is a process that decreases the entropy of the system (a bunch of molecules held in a lattice as a solid have way fewer accessible microstates than molecules that can freely move around as a liquid), and concentrating the ammonia makes the entropy change even more negative. We know ŒîH is negative, and now ŒîS is even more negative than it would be for a pure substance, so in order for the process to be thermodynamically spontaneous (ŒîG < 0), T (the freezing point) will be a smaller number.
(d) Temperature: about -97¬∞C, composition: accept answers between 30-40% ammonia.
Titan's subsurface ocean is maintained by tidal heating and antifreeze compounds like ammonia, allowing liquid water to exist despite the moon's cold surface temperature.
20. Open-Ended Concept Discussion (5 points total)
When preparing for this event, you probably studied some concepts that weren't covered explicitly on this exam, simply because this exam can't be infinitely long. Choose one of them and talk about it in as much detail as you can. Note: this question will be the first tiebreaker.
Answer: Here are 5 important Solar System topics explained for students:

üåå Topic 1: How Planets Form from Dust

What is it? Planets don't just appear in space - they grow from tiny pieces of dust and rock over millions of years!

How does it work? Imagine you're making a snowball. You start with a small handful of snow, then roll it around to pick up more snow until it becomes huge. Planets form the same way! Here's the process:

  • Step 1 - Dust Sticks Together: Tiny particles of dust and ice in space bump into each other and stick together, like when you rub a balloon on your hair and it picks up small pieces of paper.
  • Step 2 - Bigger Chunks Form: These dust bunnies keep growing bigger and bigger, eventually becoming rocks the size of mountains.
  • Step 3 - Gravity Takes Over: Once these chunks get big enough (about the size of a large city), their gravity becomes strong enough to pull in more material from far away.
  • Step 4 - Planets Are Born: Over millions of years, these growing balls of rock and ice become planets!

Cool Fact: The rocky planets (like Earth) formed closer to the Sun where it was too hot for ice to exist. The gas giants (like Jupiter) formed farther away where it was cold enough for ice to help them grow much bigger!

üß≤ Topic 2: Planetary Magnetospheres - Invisible Force Fields

What is it? Some planets have invisible "force fields" called magnetospheres that protect them from dangerous space radiation!

How does it work? Think of a magnetosphere like an invisible bubble around a planet. Here's what creates it:

  • Liquid Metal Core: Deep inside planets like Earth, there's liquid metal (mostly iron) that's constantly moving around.
  • Electric Currents: This moving liquid metal creates electric currents, just like the wires in your house.
  • Magnetic Field: These electric currents create a huge magnetic field around the entire planet - like a giant invisible magnet!
  • Protection: This magnetic field deflects harmful particles from the Sun, keeping them from hitting the planet's surface.

Why is this important? Without Earth's magnetosphere, the Sun's radiation would slowly strip away our atmosphere, and life as we know it couldn't exist. Mars lost most of its magnetosphere billions of years ago, which is one reason why it lost most of its atmosphere!

Cool Fact: The Northern Lights (Aurora) happen when some of the Sun's particles do get through Earth's magnetosphere and hit our atmosphere, creating beautiful dancing lights in the sky!

üíç Topic 3: Why Saturn Has Rings (And How They Stay There)

What is it? Saturn's rings are made of billions of pieces of ice and rock, some as small as snowballs and others as big as houses!

How did they form? Scientists think Saturn's rings formed in one of these ways:

  • Broken Moon Theory: A moon got too close to Saturn and was torn apart by Saturn's strong gravity.
  • Leftover Material: The rings might be leftover material from when Saturn was forming that never became a moon.
  • Comet Crash: A large comet might have crashed into one of Saturn's moons, creating the ring material.

Why don't the rings fall into Saturn? This is like asking why the Moon doesn't fall into Earth! The ring particles are constantly moving around Saturn very fast. Their speed creates a force that balances out Saturn's gravity trying to pull them in.

Why don't they fly away? Saturn's gravity is strong enough to keep them from escaping into space, but not so strong that they fall in.

Cool Fact: Saturn's rings are incredibly thin - if you made a scale model where the rings were as wide as a football field, they would only be as thick as a piece of paper!

üî¨ Topic 4: Astrobiology - The Search for Life in Space

What is it? Astrobiology is the study of life in the universe - both life on Earth and the possibility of life on other worlds!

Where might we find life? Scientists look for life in places that have the basic ingredients:

  • Liquid Water: All life on Earth needs water, so we look for places where water can exist as a liquid.
  • Energy Source: Life needs energy to survive - this could be sunlight, heat from inside a planet, or chemical energy.
  • Chemical Building Blocks: Life needs elements like carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen to build complex molecules.

Exciting Places to Look:

  • Europa (Jupiter's moon): Has a liquid ocean under its icy surface that might contain more water than all of Earth's oceans combined!
  • Enceladus (Saturn's moon): Shoots geysers of water into space from its subsurface ocean.
  • Mars: Once had rivers and lakes, and might still have liquid water underground.
  • Titan (Saturn's moon): Has lakes and rivers, though they're made of liquid methane instead of water.

Cool Fact: Scientists have found life on Earth in some of the most extreme places imaginable - in boiling hot springs, in ice, in acid, and even deep underground. This gives us hope that life might exist in extreme environments on other worlds too!

üöÄ Topic 5: How We Design Space Missions

What is it? Sending a spacecraft to another planet is like throwing a paper airplane from New York and trying to hit a moving target in California - while both you and the target are spinning!

The Challenge: Planets are constantly moving in their orbits, so mission planners have to figure out exactly when and where to launch a spacecraft so it will meet up with its target planet months or years later.

Launch Windows: There are only certain times when it's possible to launch to each planet:

  • Mars: Every 26 months when Earth and Mars are properly aligned
  • Jupiter: About every 13 months
  • Saturn: About every 12.5 months

Gravity Assists: This is like a cosmic slingshot! Spacecraft can use a planet's gravity to speed up and change direction without using fuel. The Voyager spacecraft used gravity assists from Jupiter and Saturn to visit all four outer planets.

Mission Types:

  • Flyby: Spacecraft zooms past the target (like New Horizons at Pluto)
  • Orbiter: Spacecraft goes into orbit around the target (like Cassini at Saturn)
  • Lander: Spacecraft lands on the surface (like the Mars rovers)
  • Sample Return: Spacecraft brings pieces of another world back to Earth (like OSIRIS-REx from asteroid Bennu)

Cool Fact: It takes radio signals from Earth about 22 minutes to reach Mars when it's closest, and up to 24 minutes to reach Mars when it's farthest away. This means mission controllers can't control Mars rovers in real-time - the rovers have to be smart enough to make decisions on their own!
