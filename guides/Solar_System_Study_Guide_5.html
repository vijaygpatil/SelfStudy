Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Questions - 1 Point Each - Total: 20 Points
1. Approximately how many confirmed exoplanets do we know of as of 2022? (order-of-magnitude)? [1 point]
a. 50
b. 5,000
c. 500
d. 50,000
e. 500,000
Answer: B - 5,000
As of 2022, there were approximately 5,000 confirmed exoplanets discovered, with the number growing rapidly thanks to missions like Kepler, TESS, and ground-based surveys.
2. In 2020, an international team led by Dr. Jane Greaves reported the discovery of phosphine gas in the atmosphere of which of the following planets? [1 point]
a. Venus
b. Jupiter
c. Neptune
d. Uranus
e. Saturn
f. Earth
Answer: A - Venus
Dr. Jane Greaves and her team reported the detection of phosphine (PH‚ÇÉ) in Venus' atmosphere, which was significant because phosphine is considered a potential biosignature since it's difficult to produce through non-biological processes in Venus' harsh environment.
3. In 2019, water vapor was discovered in the atmosphere of an exoplanet for the first time ever. Which exoplanet was it? [1 point]
a. Kepler 138d
b. K2-33b
c. K2-18b
d. None of the Above
Answer: C - K2-18b
K2-18b was the first exoplanet where water vapor was detected in its atmosphere in 2019. This super-Earth orbits in the habitable zone of its red dwarf star, making the water vapor detection particularly exciting for astrobiology.
4. True or False: most known "Hot Jupiters" orbit at an average distance of less than one astronomical unit (1 AU) from their host star. [1 point]
True
False
Answer: True
Hot Jupiters are gas giant planets that orbit very close to their host stars, typically much closer than Mercury orbits our Sun (0.39 AU). Most Hot Jupiters orbit at distances of 0.01 to 0.1 AU from their stars, which is why they have such high temperatures.
5. Approximately how many moons of Jupiter are known to date? (note that scientists sometimes disagree on the exact answer to the first question so choose the closest answer. Currently unnamed moons count as "known" for sake of this question) [1 point]
a. 53
b. 79
c. 118
d. 47
e. 91
f. 71
Answer: B - 79
As of 2022, Jupiter has approximately 79 known moons, including the four large Galilean moons (Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto) and many smaller irregular moons. The exact number changes as new small moons are discovered and confirmed.
6. A "catena" is a type of surface feature found on rocky bodies in the Solar System. Which of the following is the best definition for this term? [1 point]
a. low-elevation plain
b. canyon
c. crater chain
d. type of volcanic plume
e. ice geyser
f. None of the above
Answer: C - crater chain
A catena is a chain or line of craters, often formed when a comet or asteroid breaks apart before impact, creating multiple impacts in a row. The word "catena" comes from Latin meaning "chain."
7. Jupiter's atmosphere is primarily dominated by which gas? [1 point]
a. Hydrogen gas (H‚ÇÇ)
b. Ammonia
c. Methane
d. Carbon Dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ)
e. Helium gas (He)
f. None of the above
Answer: A - Hydrogen gas (H‚ÇÇ)
Jupiter's atmosphere is about 89% hydrogen and 10% helium, with trace amounts of other gases like methane, ammonia, and water vapor. This composition is similar to the Sun and reflects Jupiter's formation from the primordial solar nebula.
8. Select the statements that are true about the "Grand Tack model." (choose multiple if necessary; must get exactly correct to earn the point) [1 point]
a. Jupiter formed at a greater distance from the Sun than its current position
b. Jupiter once migrated inward toward the Sun
c. Jupiter once migrated outward away from the Sun
d. The Grand Tack model successfully explains the relatively small mass of Mars
e. None of the above are true
Answer: B, C, D - Jupiter once migrated inward toward the Sun; Jupiter once migrated outward away from the Sun; The Grand Tack model successfully explains the relatively small mass of Mars
The Grand Tack model proposes that Jupiter first migrated inward to about 1.5 AU, then "tacked" (like a sailboat) and migrated outward to its current position. This migration scattered material that would have formed Mars, explaining why Mars is much smaller than expected.
9. The "Cassini Division" separates which two rings of Saturn? Also, True or False: the "F Ring" is Saturn's innermost ring. [1 point]
a. F and A rings; True
b. F and A rings; False
c. A and B rings; True
d. A and B rings; False
e. D and F rings; True
f. D and F rings; False
Answer: D - A and B rings; False
The Cassini Division is the large gap between Saturn's A and B rings. The F Ring is not the innermost ring - the D ring is Saturn's innermost ring, followed by C, B, A, and then F ring moving outward.
10. What is the approximate angle between the ecliptic plane, on which most of the Solar System's planets lie, and the Galactic plane? [1 point]
a. 0 degrees
b. 30 degrees
c. 60 degrees
d. 90 degrees
Answer: C - 60 degrees
The ecliptic plane (the plane of Earth's orbit around the Sun) is tilted about 60 degrees relative to the galactic plane (the plane of the Milky Way galaxy). This means our Solar System is oriented at a significant angle to our galaxy's disk.
11. Rank the following planets by density, from most dense to least dense: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars [1 point]
a. Mars, Venus, Mercury, Earth
b. Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars
c. Mars, Earth, Venus, Mercury
d. Earth, Venus, Mercury, Mars
e. Earth, Mercury, Venus, Mars
f. None of the above
Answer: D - Earth, Venus, Mercury, Mars
Earth has the highest density (5.52 g/cm¬≥), followed by Venus (5.24 g/cm¬≥), Mercury (5.43 g/cm¬≥), and Mars (3.93 g/cm¬≥). Earth's high density comes from its large iron core and gravitational compression.
12. True or False: Earth's moon has a smaller diameter than Pluto. [1 point]
True
False
Answer: False
Earth's Moon has a diameter of 3,474 km, while Pluto has a diameter of 2,374 km. The Moon is actually larger than Pluto, making it one of the largest moons in the Solar System relative to its planet.
13. What mechanism best explains Io's high rate of volcano activity? [1 point]
a. Tidal heating
b. Meteor Impacts
c. Tectonic plate movement
d. Warming of ice causing an increase in nitrogen gas pressure
e. None of the Above
Answer: A - Tidal heating
Io's intense volcanic activity is caused by tidal heating from Jupiter's immense gravitational pull. As Io orbits Jupiter in an elliptical orbit, the changing gravitational forces stretch and compress the moon, generating heat through friction in its interior.
14. Many experiments mapping the interiors of planets (for example, NASA's InSight Mission on Mars) rely on measuring time delays between "P" and "S" waves. Which type is faster, and what does the "S" stand for? [1 point]
a. P-waves are faster; S stands for "seismic"
b. P-waves are faster; S stands for "secondary"
c. P-waves are slower; S stands for "seismic"
d. P-waves are slower; S stands for "secondary"
Answer: B - P-waves are faster; S stands for "secondary"
P-waves (primary waves) travel faster than S-waves (secondary waves) through planetary interiors. P-waves are compression waves that can travel through both solids and liquids, while S-waves are shear waves that only travel through solids.
15. Arrokoth is a/an _____. (select multiple answers if necessary) [1 point]
a. Kuiper Belt Object
b. Trans-Neptunian Object
c. Contact Binary
d. Interstellar Object
e. Object originally discovered by the New Horizons spacecraft
Answer: A, B, C - Kuiper Belt Object; Trans-Neptunian Object; Contact Binary
Arrokoth (formerly 2014 MU69) is a Kuiper Belt Object and Trans-Neptunian Object that has a contact binary shape (two lobes touching). It was visited by New Horizons but was discovered by the Hubble Space Telescope, not New Horizons itself.
16. Which of the following is NOT one of the four Galilean moons of Jupiter? [1 point]
a. Io
b. Titan
c. Callisto
d. Europa
e. Ganymede
Answer: B - Titan
Titan is Saturn's largest moon, not one of Jupiter's moons. The four Galilean moons of Jupiter are Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto, discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610.
17. Which of the following distance ranges best describes the position of the asteroid belt in our Solar System? [1 point]
a. 6.0 AU to 7.0 AU from the Sun
b. 1.8 AU to 2.8 AU from the Sun
c. 5.3 AU to 6.3 AU from the Sun
d. 2.8 AU to 3.8 AU from the Sun
e. 3.2 AU to 4.2 AU from the Sun
f. 2.2 AU to 3.2 AU from the Sun
Answer: F - 2.2 AU to 3.2 AU from the Sun
The main asteroid belt is located between Mars (1.5 AU) and Jupiter (5.2 AU), roughly spanning from 2.2 to 3.2 AU from the Sun. This region contains most of the known asteroids in our Solar System.
18. Rank the following planets by their magnetic field strength (from strongest to weakest): Jupiter, Earth, Venus [1 point]
a. Earth, Jupiter, Venus
b. Earth, Venus, Jupiter
c. Venus, Earth, Jupiter
d. Jupiter, Earth, Venus
e. Jupiter, Venus, Earth
Answer: D - Jupiter, Earth, Venus
Jupiter has the strongest magnetic field in the Solar System, about 20,000 times stronger than Earth's. Earth has a moderate magnetic field generated by its liquid iron core. Venus has essentially no magnetic field due to its slow rotation and lack of a magnetic dynamo.
19. Pluto revolves around the Sun with an orbital period of 248 years. During how much of this time is Pluto closer to the Sun than Neptune (approximately)? [1 point]
a. 0 years; Pluto is always further to the Sun than Neptune
b. ~10 years
c. ~20 years
d. ~30 years
e. ~40 years
f. ~80 years
Answer: C - ~20 years
Due to Pluto's highly elliptical orbit, it comes closer to the Sun than Neptune for about 20 years out of its 248-year orbital period. This last happened from 1979 to 1999, when Pluto was temporarily the 8th planet from the Sun.
20. Uranus' moons are generally named after which of the following categories? [1 point]
a. water/sea deities
b. Shakespeare characters
c. lovers or descendants of Zeus
d. characters with ties to Hades
e. None of the above
Answer: B - Shakespeare characters
Uranus' moons are uniquely named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope, rather than mythological figures like most other planetary moons. Examples include Titania, Oberon, Miranda, Ariel, and Umbriel.

32. The image below shows the distribution of the orbital semi-major axes of objects in the asteroid belt. The vertical dashed lines mark the so-called "Kirkwood Gaps". What phenomenon causes these mathematically-predictable gaps? (your answer should be just a single sentence). This question is worth 3 points and will be a tiebreaker question. [3 points]

Note: This question refers to a graph showing asteroid distribution with gaps at certain orbital distances.

Answer: Orbital resonances with Jupiter
Kirkwood Gaps are caused by orbital resonances with Jupiter, where asteroids at certain distances have orbital periods that are simple fractions of Jupiter's orbital period (like 1:2, 1:3, etc.). Jupiter's gravitational influence gradually removes asteroids from these resonant orbits, creating the gaps.

35. The image from the previous question shows the star obscured by a black circle on the image. What is the name of the type of tool/instrument that creates this feature (hint: it starts with a "c") and why does it help us image exoplanets? (1pt for the tool's name; 1 pt for purpose - you can probably answer the second part without knowing the first). This type of tool/instrument is a very common tool for direct imaging of exoplanets. [2 points]
Answer: Coronagraph; blocks out light from star so makes it easier to see planets
A coronagraph is an instrument that blocks out the bright light from a star, allowing much fainter objects like exoplanets to be seen. Without a coronagraph, the star's light would completely overwhelm the faint light reflected by orbiting planets.

36. Explain why Pluto was demoted from planet status (3 points). [3 points]
Answer:
Pluto was reclassified as a "dwarf planet" in 2006 because it doesn't meet all three criteria for being a planet: (1) It orbits the Sun ‚úì, (2) It has enough mass to be roughly round ‚úì, but (3) It has not cleared its orbital neighborhood ‚úó. Pluto shares its orbital space with many other objects in the Kuiper Belt and cannot gravitationally dominate its orbit like the eight planets do.
37. We often see false-color images of Solar System bodies. Explain what a "false-color" image is (3 points). [3 points]

For reference: A false-color image uses colors that are different from what the human eye would see, often to highlight specific features or wavelengths of light.

Answer:
A false-color image is one where the colors shown are not the same as what the human eye would see. Scientists often take images in different wavelengths of light (like infrared, ultraviolet, or radio waves) that are invisible to humans, then assign visible colors to represent these wavelengths. This helps reveal features, temperatures, or compositions that wouldn't be visible in regular photographs. For example, a "red" area might actually represent hot temperatures detected in infrared light.

(c) Radial velocity method
(d) Transit method
(e) Lower mass stars are more easily moved by the gravitational pull of their planets, creating a larger wobble that's easier to detect.

40. Answer the following questions related to Kepler's Laws and gravitational interactions between objects. 8 points total. [8 points total]
(a, b, and c) In simple terms, explain Kepler's three laws of planetary motion. (6 points, 2 for each law)
(d) Fill in the blank. The force of gravity acting between any two objects is _______ proportional to the ______ of the separation distance between the object's centers. (2 points, 1 pt each for each blank)
Answers:
(a) First Law: Planets orbit the Sun in elliptical (oval-shaped) paths, not perfect circles
(b) Second Law: Planets move faster when they are closer to the Sun and slower when they are farther away
(c) Third Law: The time it takes a planet to orbit the Sun is related to its distance from the Sun - planets farther away take longer to complete their orbits
(d) inversely; square
41. Answer the following questions related to Planetary Migration (10 pts). [10 points total]
(a) What is Planetary Migration? (2)
(b) Disk migration arises from the gravitational force exerted by a sufficiently massive body embedded in a disk on the surrounding disk's gas, which perturbs its density distribution. Provide one subtype of disk migration and explain what is notable about the indicated subtype. (4)
(c) Aside from Disk migration, what is another type of planetary migration? (2)
(d) What is one type of object from the rules that is generally produced by planetary migration? (2)
Answers:
(a) Planetary migration is the process by which planets change their orbital distances from their host star over time due to gravitational interactions.
(b) Type I migration: affects smaller planets and causes them to spiral inward toward their star relatively quickly. Type II migration: affects larger planets that can create gaps in the disk and migrate more slowly.

42. Answer the following questions about tidal forces in the Earth/Moon/Sun system: [8 points total]
(a) What is a tidal force? (2)
(b) What characterizes a spring tide? (2)
(c) What position are the Sun, Moon, and Earth when spring tide occurs? (1)
(d) What characterizes a neap tide? (2)
(e) What position are the Sun, Moon, and Earth when neap tide occurs? (1)
Answers:
(a) A tidal force is the difference in gravitational pull on different parts of an object, caused by the fact that gravity is stronger on the side closer to the source of gravity.
(b) Spring tides have the highest high tides and the lowest low tides - the greatest difference between high and low tide levels.
(c) The Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (either in a line during new moon or full moon)
(d) Neap tides have the smallest difference between high and low tides - the high tides are lower and the low tides are higher than average.
(e) The Sun and Moon are at right angles (90 degrees) to each other as seen from Earth (during first quarter or third quarter moon)
