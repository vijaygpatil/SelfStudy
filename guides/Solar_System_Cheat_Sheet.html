Advanced Analysis (96 pts)

Venus Transit (18 pts)

  • 59a. Duration: 6-8 hours
  • 59b. Limb darkening: Star center brighter than edges due to temperature gradients; creates curved light curve
  • 59c. Without limb darkening: Flat bottom curve
  • 59d. Venus appears 3.6× larger: Much closer to Earth than Sun
  • 59e. Bright exoplanet: Underestimate radius
  • 59f. Atmospheric composition: Starlight through atmosphere reveals absorption lines

Detection Biases (16 pts)

  • 60a. RV fewer >6000K: Fewer spectral lines, more massive stars = smaller signals
  • 60b. RV fewer <4500K: Fainter stars, mainly IR emission
  • 60c. Transit fewer >6500K: Too bright/large, shallow transits
  • 60d. Transit fewer <4000K: Too faint for detection

Planet Formation (20 pts)

  • 61a. Growth rate: Faster at small distances
  • 61b. Physics: Higher surface density = more collisions
  • 61c. High density: Yes, faster growth
  • 61d. 3 AU discontinuity: Frost line where volatiles condense
  • 61e. Hot Jupiters: Formed beyond frost line, migrated inward

Jupiter Interior (22 pts)

  • 62a. Not spherical: Rapid rotation causes flattening
  • 62b. Gravitational fields: Non-spherical shape creates field variations revealing interior structure
  • 62c. Higher-order moments: More sensitive to surface structure
  • 62d. Large energy cost: Moving heavy elements from center requires enormous energy
  • 62e. Metallic hydrogen: Extreme pressure breaks H₂, creates liquid protons + mobile electrons
  • 62f. No heavy core: Supports disk instability model

Tidal Heating (20 pts)

  • 63a. Tidal heating: Jupiter's gravity + orbital resonance keeps elliptical orbit, constant deformation generates heat
  • 63b. Less temperature dependent: Radioactivity
  • 63c. Circular orbit: Reduces deformation/heating, eliminates high-T equilibrium
  • 63d. Stable equilibrium: Heat removal increases more than generation when T rises
  • 63e. Stability criterion: Heat removal slope > heat generation slope

Key Mission Data

New Horizons: Pluto flyby, Alice (UV) & Ralph (visible/IR) instruments
Cassini: Saturn orbiter (13 years), studied rings & moons
Juno: Jupiter orbiter, studying interior structure
Magellan: Venus radar mapping (1990-1994)
Voyager 2: Grand tour, only spacecraft to visit Uranus & Neptune
Galileo: Jupiter orbiter (1995-2003)
TESS: Transit Exoplanet Survey Satellite
ALMA: Atacama Large Millimeter Array

Detection Methods

Transit: Planet blocks starlight (TESS, Kepler)
Radial Velocity: Star wobbles from planet gravity
Direct Imaging: Photograph planet (HR 8799)
Limitations: Each method has stellar temperature biases

Geological Activity Indicators

Active: Smooth surfaces, few craters (Io, Triton)
Inactive: Many craters, old surfaces
Tidal Heating: Gravitational deformation (Io)
Cryovolcanism: Ice/volatile volcanism

Atmospheric Phenomena

Venus: 96% CO₂, 462°C, runaway greenhouse
Jupiter: Great Red Spot (300+ years), Oval BA
Neptune: Great Dark Spot, Small Dark Spot
Saturn: Auroras from solar wind interaction

Formation Concepts

Core Accretion: Solid core first, then gas
Disk Instability: Direct gravitational collapse
Frost Line: ~3 AU, where volatiles condense
Migration: Planets move inward after formation
Planetesimals: Grow faster closer to star (higher density)

Observational Techniques

False-color: Non-visible wavelengths in visible colors
UV/IR: Reveal composition & atmospheric depths
Spectroscopy: Analyze light for composition
Transit Photometry: Brightness changes during transits
Limb Darkening: Star center brighter than edges
